Have you ever gone to the doctor and they spent hours looking at your X-rays searching for a fracture or checking if everything is okay? My AI model has the objective of solving this problem. It received data training which should make it capable of detecting the differences between a healthy bone X-ray and a broken bone X-ray.

My model was trained to be capable of differentiating between broken and healthy arms and legs. It considered four classes of X-ray images (Broken Leg, Healthy Leg, Broken Arm, and Healthy Arm), each with around twelve samples. However, even though it had data training, it still confused a jellyfish with a healthy leg.

Why did such a problem occur? This type of problem happens because AI models require massive amounts of data in order to be capable of making accurate decisions. In addition, the model got confused because the jellyfish image had the same color palette as an X-ray image and probably confused the tentacles with a human leg. Solving this problem would require the input of many more samples along with stress testing the model.

Moreover, even if an actual arm or leg X-ray were inputted and given more data training, the program could still fail. This is caused by the fact that perfect, identical conditions do not exist, so the model could still get confused if there is a slight change in position or lighting.

Nevertheless, the program would definitely present better outputs after undergoing diverse tests and with the input of more image samples. For this reason, AI models are still good and can be extremely useful, saving a lot of time and preventing unnecessary work. Furthermore, the time taken by a doctor to analyze an X-ray could be drastically reduced with the help of an AI program, especially because the doctor would have the knowledge required to check if the model’s answer is correct, ensuring that the patient receives the best treatment.
