## What is an AP course

AP stands for Advanced Placement, a program run by the College Board that lets high school students take college-level courses and sit a national exam in May. AP courses are developed jointly by college faculty and experienced high school teachers, who work together to set the curriculum, write the exam, and establish scoring standards.  Each exam is scored from 1 to 5. A qualifying score can make a student eligible, once in college, to receive course credit, placement into more advanced courses, or both.  The specific score a college accepts varies by institution, so credit policies differ from one school to the next.


## What is AP CSP

AP Computer Science Principles (AP CSP) is an introductory, college-level computing course equivalent to a first-semester course for non-majors. Unlike AP Computer Science A, it does not require learning a specific programming language, and focuses instead on computational thinking, creativity, and how computing affects society.  No prior coding experience is needed.  Students learn to design and evaluate solutions, write programs using algorithms and abstraction, work with data to draw conclusions, and examine how computing innovations (including the internet) work and what their benefits and harms are. Assessment has two parts: the Create performance task, a program students build during the course and submit through the AP Digital Portfolio, and a digital end-of-course exam of 70 multiple-choice questions plus two written-response questions tied to that task.


## What you will learn in AP CSP

By the end of the course, you will be able to:

- **Design and build programs.** Plan, write, and test programs using algorithms, loops, conditionals, variables, and lists, and break problems into smaller parts using abstraction.
- **Work with data.** Collect, organize, and analyze data to find patterns and draw conclusions, and understand how computers represent and store information.
- **Understand the internet and computing systems.** Explain how the internet works, how data moves across networks, and how the systems behind everyday technology are built.
- **Examine the impact of computing.** Evaluate how computing innovations affect people and society, weighing both their benefits and their harms, including questions of privacy, bias, and access.
- **Collaborate and communicate.** Work with others on computing projects and explain your ideas and design choices clearly, in writing and in person.

No prior coding experience is needed. The course builds these skills gradually, and the programming you do can be in any language that supports the core building blocks of computing.


## Course structure: the Big Ideas

AP CSP is organized into five Big Ideas:

1. Creative Development
  - How to design, test, and refine software through collaboration and iterative development.
    
2. Data
  - How digital systems represent, compress, visualize, and secure real-world information.
    
3. Algorithms and Programming
   - How to determine the efficiency of algorithms,as well as writing and implementing algorithms in a program.
     
4. Computer Systems and Networks
   - How computer systems and networks, primarily the Internet, work, and how information is transmitted on the Internet and the safeguards that have been put in place to keep this system from breaking down.
     
5. Impact of Computing
   - Explore the impacts of creating computer programs, affecting socieites, economies and cultures, the legal and ethical concerns that come with programs, and the responsibilities of programmers


## The Performance Task

A portion of the AP CSP score comes from the Create performance task, a programming project students build during the course rather than on exam day. Students are given 9 hours of in-class time to develop a program of their own choosing, record a video of it running, and prepare a Personalized Project Reference documenting their code.  The program and its materials are submitted as final through the AP Digital Portfolio. 

The task is designed to show what a student can build and explain, not just what they can recall. On the end-of-course exam, students answer two written-response questions about the code in their own Personalized Project Reference, so the project and the exam are directly connected.  The program can be written in any language that supports the core building blocks of computing, which means students choose tools that fit their idea.


## You may also be interested in: AP CSA

AP Computer Science A (AP CSA) is the school's other AP computer science course. Where AP CSP is a broad, conceptual introduction with no required programming language, AP CSA is more programming-focused and teaches a subset of Java. The two can be taken in any order, and neither requires the other first. If you want to go deeper into writing code, AP CSA is the natural next step.
